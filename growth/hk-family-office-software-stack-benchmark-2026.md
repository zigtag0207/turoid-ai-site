# HK Family Office Software Stack Benchmark 2026

Prepared by Turoid

## Executive Summary

Hong Kong family offices rarely wake up with a clean software buying journey. The need usually appears through recurring operating pain:

- Monthly reporting depends on too many bank files, fund PDFs, and Excel workbooks.
- Alternative investments create document-heavy workflows around capital calls, distributions, NAVs, manager letters, and commitment tracking.
- Investment teams want AI, but do not yet have controlled source data, review queues, or permissioned workflows.
- Principals and CIOs want faster answers, but the operating team still spends too much time preparing the evidence.

This benchmark gives a simple operating lens for evaluating whether a family office software stack is ready for reporting, alternatives, AI document workflows, and investment decision support.

## The Five-Layer Benchmark

### 1. Portfolio Reporting Layer

Good family office software should give the team a trusted portfolio view without forcing every month-end pack through manual copy-and-paste work.

Benchmark questions:

- Can the office see consolidated exposure across banks, custodians, private funds, and manually tracked assets?
- Are positions, cash, transactions, and performance checks traceable back to their sources?
- Can recurring reports be regenerated with fewer one-off spreadsheet changes?
- Can the team explain what changed between reporting periods?

Warning signs:

- A senior team member is the only person who knows how the monthly reporting workbook works.
- Private-bank data is checked visually instead of through repeatable reconciliation logic.
- Reporting packs are rebuilt every month instead of refreshed from controlled data.

### 2. Excel Risk Layer

Excel is still useful for analysis. The risk begins when Excel becomes the operating system.

Benchmark questions:

- Which spreadsheets are analytical, and which spreadsheets are operational controls?
- Which files contain decision records, approvals, exceptions, or critical calculations?
- Can the team recover quickly if the owner of a workbook leaves?
- Are spreadsheet changes reviewed or logged?

Warning signs:

- The most important file is named with dates, initials, or "final final".
- Approvals live in email while supporting numbers live in separate spreadsheets.
- There is no clear source of truth after a report is sent.

### 3. Alternative Investment Operations Layer

Alternatives are often the highest-friction asset class for family offices because the workflow is document-heavy and event-driven.

Benchmark questions:

- Can the office track commitments, funded capital, unfunded capital, NAVs, distributions, and IRR by manager and vehicle?
- Are capital calls and distribution notices connected to evidence documents?
- Can manager letters and fund reports be searched, summarized, and linked to holdings?
- Can the team identify missing documents before reporting deadlines?

Warning signs:

- Capital calls, NAVs, and manager letters are tracked across email, folders, and spreadsheet tabs.
- Private fund data is updated only when someone remembers to check the inbox.
- The team cannot quickly answer where a number in the report came from.

### 4. AI Document Processing Layer

AI becomes valuable when it is connected to a controlled workflow, not when it is treated as a generic chatbot.

Benchmark questions:

- Which documents repeat every month or quarter?
- Can AI extract structured fields with source links and human review?
- Can exceptions be routed to the right person instead of buried in a chat history?
- Can the system retain evidence, confidence, and approval trails?

Useful starting documents:

- Private-bank statements
- Capital call notices
- Distribution notices
- Fund reports
- Manager letters
- Contract notes
- Investment committee materials

Warning signs:

- AI output is copied into Excel without source evidence.
- The office cannot separate approved data from AI suggestions.
- There is no review workflow for low-confidence extraction.

### 5. Governance And Control Layer

Family office software should make the team faster without weakening control.

Benchmark questions:

- Are approvals captured in the same workflow as the underlying data?
- Can users see who changed what and why?
- Are permissions designed around roles, portfolios, and workflows?
- Can the office hand over responsibilities without losing institutional memory?

Warning signs:

- Exceptions are resolved verbally or in message threads.
- Approval records are detached from the portfolio data they relate to.
- Access rights are broad because the workflow is too manual to split cleanly.

## Maturity Scorecard

Score each layer from 1 to 5.

1. Manual and person-dependent
2. Partially documented but spreadsheet-heavy
3. Repeatable process with some controlled data
4. Workflow-driven with evidence and review
5. Integrated system with reporting, controls, and AI assistance

Recommended interpretation:

- 5-10: Stabilize the reporting and data workflow first.
- 11-16: Target the highest-friction recurring workflow for a 30-day pilot.
- 17-22: Add document AI and exception handling to reduce manual review.
- 23-25: Move toward AI-assisted CIO workflows grounded in controlled data.

## The First 30-Day Pilot

The best first pilot is usually narrow:

- One recurring report
- One document type
- One private fund workflow
- One approval flow
- One set of portfolio questions

Good pilot criteria:

- It happens every month or quarter.
- It has a clear owner.
- It currently consumes manual time.
- It has measurable output.
- It can be improved without replacing the whole stack.

Example pilots:

- Turn a monthly private-bank statement workflow into a reviewed data pipeline.
- Extract capital call and distribution details into a structured alternatives register.
- Build an evidence-backed monthly portfolio report refresh workflow.
- Create an AI assistant for manager letter search and briefing notes.
- Add approval and exception tracking to a recurring trade or reporting workflow.
